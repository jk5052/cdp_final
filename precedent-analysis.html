Who Defines Emotions?

Elisa Giardina Papa's "Cleaning Emotional Data" (2020)

Through screens, textile works, and installation elements in the gallery space, the work visualizes the process of emotional data labeling. It juxtaposes the abstract lines of facial expressions extracted by algorithms with untranslatable emotional expressions from Sicilian and American dialects. Waste materials are transformed into a new canvas for "unruly and incomputable" emotions.

This anthropological study explores the linguistic gaps and differences in interpretative parameters between human-machine relationships. As the final work in a trilogy following "Technologies of Care" (2016) and "Labor of Sleep" (2017), it analyzes the separation between material places and virtual labor spaces in the platform economy.

The work serves as a record of microwork that the artist herself actually performed remotely for American companies‚Äîevidence of labeling, categorizing, annotating, and validating data for training emotion recognition algorithms. It stands as first-person testimony to new forms of precarious labor.

Tasks performed included: systematizing emotions, annotating facial expressions, and recording her own image for 3D character animation. Some of her emotional expression videos were rejected because her facial expressions did not fully match "standardized" emotional categories.

Critical perspective: AI systems based on flawed understandings that view human emotions as universal, authentic, and transparent. Tech companies and government agencies leverage this prescribed transparency to develop software that identifies both consumers' moods and potentially dangerous citizens who pose threats to the state.

Artist's First-Person Experience: Autoethnographic Methodology

Through autoethnographic methodology, the artist:

  • Documents the linguistic and cultural gaps experienced while residing in Sicily and collaborating with American companies
  • Exposes the hidden labor structures of the AI industry through actual microworker experiences
  • Analyzes power relations in the global platform economy through personal experience

Western-Centricity of Six "Universal" Basic Emotions

The work reveals the limitations of Western-centric emotional classification systems through the contrast between "abstract facial expression lines" extracted by algorithms and "untranslatable Sicilian/American dialect expressions." This suggests that the concept of emotional "universality" itself is a construct based on specific cultural contexts.

History of Emotional Data

Colonial Genealogy of Emotion AI

  • Stage 1: Colonial Origins (1872-1960s)

    Darwin (1872): Evolutionary justification of "universal emotions" concept

    Ekman (1960s-70s): Research on Papua New Guinea's Fore people as "isolated culture"

    Core: Western scholars extracting "pure" emotional data from "primitive" societies

  • Stage 2: Technical Translation (1980s-2000s)

    FACS (1980): Systematizing Ekman's six basic emotions into coding systems

    Affective Computing (1997): MIT's transformation of emotions into computable data

    Core: Western academic institutions translating "universality" into algorithms

  • Stage 3: Global Exploitation (2000s-Present)

    Affectiva (2009): Commercializing MIT technology, utilizing Cairo crowdworkers

    Big Tech Expansion: Amazon, Microsoft, Google commercializing emotion AI

    Core: Global South labor creating Western corporate profits

  • Stage 4: Scientific Refutation (2019-Present)

    Barrett et al. (2019): Scientific proof that "emotions cannot be predicted from faces"

    Racial bias studies: AI interpreting Black faces more negatively

    Core: Dismantling 150 years of "universality" myth, yet industry continues

Continuity

Evidence of Racial Bias: AI Systems Interpreting Black Faces More Negatively

Lauren Rhue's analysis of 400 NBA players' official photos found that Face++ rated Black faces as twice as angry as white faces on average, while Microsoft Face API scored Black faces as three times more contemptuous than white faces.

Specific case: Both Darren Collison and Gordon Hayward were smiling with similar smile scores (48.7 and 48.1), but Hayward was rated 59.7% happy/0.13% angry while Collison was rated 39.2% happy/27% angry.

Hybrid Media Ecology - Multiple Videos + Large-Scale Textile Works

Installation Components:

  • Multiple screen installation: Monitors at different heights and angles occupying space
  • Textile work "Amiss Motifs": Fabric work created in collaboration with Michael Graham
  • Juxtaposition of abstract lines of facial micro-expressions detected by algorithms with untranslatable emotional vernacular from Sicilian dialect and American English

They form a hybrid media ecology:

  • Digital-analog intersection: Coexistence of digital data on screens and tactile physicality of textiles
  • Layered temporality: Real-time algorithmic processing vs. slow time of handicraft
  • Spatial reconfiguration: Non-linear narrative experienced as viewers move between screens and textiles

Visual Language: Triple Function of the Artist's Face

  • Face as Worker:

    Actual work scenes filmed during microwork performance

    Subject of emotional labeling while simultaneously being labeled object

  • Face as Data:

    Expressions reduced to "abstract lines" by algorithms

    Humanity converted into computable information

  • Face as Subject of Resistance:

    Medium expressing untranslatable emotions

    Existence that disrupts the system's classification schemes

Preserving Emotions That Failed Digital Classification Through Physical Embroidery

Meaning of "Amiss Motifs" work:

  • "Amiss" (wrong, inappropriate): Emotions that algorithms failed to classify
  • "Motifs": Reconfiguring these into new patterns

Function as Archive:

  • Limitations of digital memory: Non-standardized elements deleted as "errors"
  • Possibilities of textile memory: Tactile, material recording medium
  • Collective memory: Process of individual emotions accumulating into patterns

Textiles sampled from remnants of commercially sold bolts‚Äîtypically discarded because patterns became distorted, uneven, or broken.
